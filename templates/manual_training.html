1. How this curriculum works

Four weeks, structured as follows:

  • Week 1 (Days 1–5) — Orientation and supervised review. Goal: confidently clear a tier-1 queue.
  • Week 2 — Open items + force-match. Goal: handle stale items and ambiguous cases.
  • Week 3 — Certificates and reporting. Goal: prepare a month-end certificate end-to-end.
  • Week 4 — Independent operation. Goal: carry the desk for one full day with supervisor available but not active.

Every day has three parts:

  1. Concept check — 10-minute briefing on a specific concept.
  2. Hands-on exercise — guided activity on the real system (or the staging instance during the pilot).
  3. Reflection — 5-minute sit-down with the supervisor. What did you do, what was hard, what's next?
For supervisors. Block 90 minutes a day for the new hire in Week 1, 45 minutes in Week 2, 30 minutes in Weeks 3–4. Most of the learning is doing; your job is to answer the question that blocks them from moving forward.

2. Pre-requisites for the new hire

  • Basic reconciliation literacy — understand what a SWIFT statement is (MT940/950 or the newer ISO 20022 camt.053/camt.054), what a core-banking GL posting is, and why they need to be compared.
  • Working laptop with a modern browser (Chrome / Edge / Firefox).
  • Microsoft Authenticator installed on a smartphone.
  • Corporate email active — for receiving the enrollment link and SLA alerts.
  • Read the Overview manual before Day 1. ~20 minutes. /manuals/overview.

3. Pre-requisites for the supervisor

  • Admin or Ops role on Kilter. You will be creating accounts and setting scope.
  • A training session — a session that's safe to practise on. Either (a) a closed but un-archived production session, or (b) a session in a staging instance.
  • A blank notebook for the new hire to take notes. Surprisingly, hand-written notes stick better than Ctrl+F.

4. Day 1 — Orientation, access, first login

Concept check (30 min)

Walk the new hire through the Overview manual in person. Focus on:

  • The four roles and which one they've been assigned.
  • The "day in the life" timeline. Most of the automation runs without them.
  • The concept of a session vs an open item. Draw it on paper if it helps.

Hands-on exercise 1.1 — First login

  1. Supervisor: go to Users, click + New user. Enter new hire's details. Role = ops.
  2. Copy the enrollment URL. Share it with the new hire over chat.
  3. New hire: open the URL, scan the QR, enter the first 6-digit code.
  4. New hire: go to /login, sign in. Confirm they land on the dashboard.

Hands-on exercise 1.2 — Dashboard tour

  1. Point at each stat counter. Ask: "What does 'Pending decisions' mean?" (Answer: assignments awaiting your confirm/reject.)
  2. Walk through the three charts. Hover bars on the sparkline.
  3. Press Ctrl+K. Type "cash". Press Enter on Cash accounts. Explain why this shortcut matters.
  4. Press ?. Show the keyboard-help overlay. Close it with Esc.

Comprehension check

Ask, and listen to the answer before moving on:

  1. "What's the difference between an assignment and an open item?"
  2. "What is MFA? Why do we use it?"
  3. "If you accidentally reject a match, is it gone forever?"

Reflection (10 min)

Sit down. Ask:

  • What was clearest?
  • What's still fuzzy?
  • What's the one thing you'd like to try yourself tomorrow?

5. Day 2 — The ledger language & shadowed review

Concept check (20 min)

Today's concept: the five match tiers.

  • Draw the tiers on paper. Green → amber → orange → red → violet (split).
  • Explain: tiers are the engine's confidence, not the correctness. A tier-1 match can still be wrong. A tier-4 match can be right. Your judgement matters.
  • Walk through the Matching Engine Manual section 2.

Hands-on exercise 2.1 — Shadow a review session

Supervisor drives; new hire narrates.

  1. Open a session with at least 20 pending matches.
  2. Supervisor: open the first match. Ask new hire: "What tier is this? Why?"
  3. Supervisor: confirm or reject based on the new hire's reasoning. Correct the reasoning if wrong.
  4. Repeat for the first 10 matches. Mix tiers where possible.
  5. Hand over the keyboard. New hire takes the next 5. Supervisor narrates.

Exercise 2.2 — Writing a good note

For one of the matches reviewed in 2.1, have the new hire press N and write a note. Then review it:

  • A bad note: "looks ok"
  • An OK note: "matched despite amount diff, ref found in narration"
  • A good note: "ref ABC123 in narration; amount diff $12 is FX rounding at spot 11.2 GHS/USD; confirmed"

Rewrite until the note would help a future auditor understand the decision with no other context.

Comprehension check

  1. "What's a tier-5 match? Give an example."
  2. "You see a tier-1 match where the narration doesn't actually contain the ref. What do you do?"
  3. "Name three situations where writing a note is worth 30 seconds."

6. Days 3–5 — Solo review with spot-checks

Structure

The new hire drives the review queue. The supervisor is available but not in the same room. Check in three times a day.

Exercise 3 — Solo queue

  1. At 09:00, open a session assigned by the supervisor.
  2. Work the queue using Space / X / J / N.
  3. When a match is genuinely ambiguous, skip it rather than guess. Note the row number.
  4. At 12:00, 15:00, 17:00 — supervisor spot-check. New hire shows three decisions, explains the reasoning.

Exercise 3.2 — Reject-and-force

One supervised exercise each day: find a match where the engine paired the wrong rows, reject it, then use the Register tab to force-match the correct partner.

  1. Scroll the register. Look for a row the engine should have matched but didn't.
  2. Reject the wrong pairing.
  3. Tick the two correct rows. Click Force-match. Write a reason note.
  4. Verify the audit log captured both actions — reject then manual-match.

Comprehension check at end of Day 5

  1. "How fast are you through a 100-row queue now?" (aim: under 20 minutes for a mostly tier-1 queue)
  2. "What did you skip? Walk me through why."
  3. "What's the most surprising thing you've seen this week?"

Week 1 sign-off

The new hire passes Week 1 when:

  • ☐ They can log in, navigate, and operate the queue unassisted.
  • ☐ They have cleared at least 200 pending matches with < 5% supervisor-reversal rate.
  • ☐ They have written at least 5 notes rated "good" by the supervisor.
  • ☐ They understand what a skip vs a reject actually means.

7. Week 2 — Open items, carry-forward, force-match

Concept check (30 min)

  • The rolling ledger: why breaks outlive sessions.
  • Carry-forward: the engine re-tries prior open items against every new session for that account.
  • The ageing buckets (0-7 / 8-30 / 31-90 / 90+) and why 90+ is a red flag.
  • Write-off: when, why, and how to write a good reason.

Exercise 4 — Age triage

Goal: clear the backlog for one access area.

  1. Go to Open items. Filter to the area you've been assigned.
  2. Sort by age descending. Start with the oldest.
  3. For each item: click History first. Read what's known.
  4. Decide: clear, comment, or write off. Document your reasoning in a note on the item.
  5. Target: 20 items cleared or resolved by end of day.

Exercise 5 — Split view & force-match

  1. Find a session with unmatched SWIFT and Flex rows that you believe should pair.
  2. Open the Split view tab.
  3. Select the candidates. Watch the amount-tie pill.
  4. Match them. Write a reason.
  5. Repeat at least three times during the week, including at least one 1:N split.

Exercise 6 — Shadowing a write-off

Supervisor picks one 90+ day open item with no clear counterpart.

  1. Discuss: is this a genuine break or timing? What evidence exists?
  2. Draft the write-off reason together. Should include: why, when the last counterpart attempt was, any email references.
  3. Execute the write-off. Observe the audit log entry.
Write-offs are serious. Until the new hire has shadowed at least 5 write-offs with the supervisor, they should not execute a write-off solo. This rule survives Week 2.

8. Week 3 — Certificates & the month-end flow

Concept check (45 min)

  • The maker-checker-approver pattern. Segregation of duties.
  • What each role does on a certificate: ops prepares, IC reviews, admin signs.
  • The difference between a draft certificate (live figures) and a signed one (frozen snapshot).
  • Walk through the Internal Control Manual section 4 — the review checklist.

Exercise 7 — Draft a certificate

  1. Pick a closed-month account. (If mid-month, use last month.)
  2. Go to Certificates. Generate a draft.
  3. Click PDF 🖨. Review every figure.
  4. Self-check: does opening balance match last month's closing? Is match rate > 95%? Are there zero pending matches?
  5. Click Prepare. Add a note: "prepared as part of training exercise 7".

Exercise 8 — IC review shadow

Supervisor (with Internal Control role) performs the review. New hire watches.

  1. Supervisor opens the prepared certificate.
  2. They walk through the Internal Control Manual checklist out loud.
  3. They perform one drill-through on an open item.
  4. They click Review — or rewrite the draft if issues are found.

Exercise 9 — Defend a certificate

New hire prepares 3 certificates for different accounts. Supervisor role-plays the reviewer. New hire must defend each figure without help.

Comprehension check end of Week 3

  1. "If you sign a certificate and next week the ledger changes, does the signed certificate change?" (Answer: no — it's frozen.)
  2. "Can you prepare and review the same certificate?" (Answer: no — different role required.)
  3. "What's the first thing a reviewer looks at?" (Answer: opening balance matches prior month's closing.)

9. Week 4 — Independent operation

Structure

The new hire runs the desk for the whole week. Supervisor is available by chat but does not sit in.

Daily rhythm

TimeTask
08:00Sign in. Scan Dashboard. Note any new at-risk callouts.
08:15Download the Daily breaks report. Glance for outliers.
08:30Open review queue. Process in tier order.
10:30Coffee + 5 min on Open items for the area you own.
11:00–16:00Review queue, open items, exception investigations.
16:00End-of-day: anything still pending gets a note or a decision. Close sessions ready for auto-close.
16:30Glance at Scheduler history — any failed jobs?

Friday review

End-of-week meeting with supervisor:

  • Week's metrics — assignments decided, open items cleared, write-offs, certificates prepared.
  • What went wrong. What got stuck. What took too long.
  • Any patterns in the breaks that might warrant a new auto-grouping rule — check Match patterns together.

10. Supervisor sign-off checklist

At the end of Week 4, the supervisor signs off the new hire as independent. Criteria:

  • ☐ Week 1 — queue literacy: confirms / rejects / notes at a steady pace with < 5% reversal rate.
  • ☐ Week 2 — open items: can triage by age and make a clear decision (clear / comment / write-off).
  • ☐ Week 2 — force-match: has executed at least one 1:1 and one 1:N manual match correctly.
  • ☐ Week 3 — certificates: has prepared at least 3 certificates that passed IC review without rework.
  • ☐ Week 4 — independent day: ran the desk for 5 consecutive days without escalations.
  • ☐ Comprehension — correctly answers 8 of 10 questions from the oral exam (see Appendix A).
  • ☐ Audit posture — the Activity log shows only clean actions. No patterns suggesting rushing or guessing.

When all boxes are ticked, the supervisor posts a note against the user record: "Training complete, independent as of YYYY-MM-DD". That's visible in the Users admin page and becomes evidence in the next compliance review.

12. Appendix A — Exercise answer key (supervisor only)

Do not share with the new hire before the oral exam. The point of the questions is the reasoning, not the memorised answer.

Oral exam — pass mark 8 of 10

  1. Q: What is the difference between an assignment and an open item?
    A: An assignment is a proposed or decided pairing inside a single session. An open item is a single-sided row that outlived its session and is now in the rolling ledger.
  2. Q: Explain the tier system in one sentence.
    A: Tiers 1–4 express engine confidence on 1:1 matches (ref + amount → ref alone → date + amount → date-tolerated); tier 5 is for ref-gated 1:N / N:1 splits.
  3. Q: If you reject a match and the two rows go back to unmatched, what happens to them at end of day?
    A: When the session closes, they become open items on the rolling ledger, auto-categorised, auto-assigned to a functional team. Future sessions may carry-forward-clear them.
  4. Q: Why can't you edit a signed certificate?
    A: Signing freezes the figures into a JSON snapshot on the certificate record. Later ledger changes don't rewrite history. This is the control property.
  5. Q: What does Ctrl+K do?
    A: Opens the command palette — a fuzzy-search overlay that jumps to any page, account, or recent session.
  6. Q: You find a 120-day-old open item with no counterpart ever arriving. What do you do?
    A: Check the history. If no counterpart in sight, escalate to supervisor for a write-off decision. Draft a reason that names the investigation, the date of last correspondent contact, and the risk.
  7. Q: What's the point of reference normalisation?
    A: Collapse formatting differences (ABC/12-3 vs ABC123 vs 000ABC123) so the same underlying reference matches regardless of which upstream system formatted it.
  8. Q: When would you use the Split view instead of the Queue tab?
    A: When you need to hunt for a counterpart visually — especially for cases where the engine didn't propose a match but you suspect one exists, or for 1:N / N:1 aggregates.
  9. Q: What's the difference between a tier-1 match and a confirmed match?
    A: Tier-1 is the engine's confidence. Confirmed is a human's decision. Every confirmed match carries its tier — the tier alone doesn't mean confirmed.
  10. Q: The scheduler is showing a red dot. What do you do?
    A: Alert IT or the admin — the automation daemon has died. Scheduled jobs aren't firing. Manual scan + close will still work, but automation needs to come back before SLA alerts miss.
